# Corrected Final Introduction

## Paper Title
Policy-Aligned Unit Commitment–Life Cycle Assessment for Renewable Energy Integration in Japan

## Introduction
Power systems are undergoing a structural transition as governments seek to reduce greenhouse gas emissions while maintaining reliable and economical electricity supply. Renewable energy technologies, including photovoltaic generation, wind power, and hydropower, are central to this transition because they can reduce dependence on fossil-fuel generation and support long-term decarbonization targets [1], [2]. However, increasing the annual share of renewable electricity is not sufficient to demonstrate that a power system can be operated reliably. High renewable penetration changes the net-load profile, increases the need for operational flexibility, and requires coordinated scheduling of thermal generators, battery energy storage, reserve provision, and renewable curtailment [3], [4]. Therefore, the transition toward a high-renewable power system should be evaluated not only from the viewpoint of installed capacity or annual generation share, but also from the viewpoint of hourly operational feasibility.

Japan provides an important case for this issue. The Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry projects renewable electricity to account for approximately 40–50% of the generation mix by fiscal year 2040, while thermal generation is expected to decrease to approximately 30–40% and nuclear power is indicated at approximately 20% in the national power-mix outlook [5], [6]. In addition, Japan’s carbon-neutrality direction requires further decarbonization of the power sector toward 2050 [7]. Several studies have evaluated Japan’s long-term energy transition pathways, renewable deployment, regional power-system transition, and net-zero scenarios at the policy or planning level [8]–[15]. These policy targets define the intended direction of the future generation mix and provide an essential basis for long-term energy planning. However, they are mainly expressed as annual or long-term energy shares. They do not directly show how the system should be operated hour by hour under demand variation, renewable intermittency, battery charging and discharging, reserve requirements, renewable curtailment, and thermal generator flexibility.

This distinction is critical. Policy ambitions are set annually, but translating these targets into stable hour-by-hour operation remains a complex challenge because generator commitment, renewable variability, battery dispatch, reserve provision, curtailment, and thermal flexibility jointly determine both operational feasibility and environmental outcomes. In actual operation, thermal generators must satisfy minimum and maximum output limits, ramping limits, startup and shutdown constraints, and minimum up/down time requirements. At the same time, photovoltaic and wind generation fluctuate with weather conditions, which may create surplus renewable generation during low-demand or high-output periods. When system flexibility is insufficient, this surplus must be curtailed or absorbed by storage. Battery energy storage can mitigate this problem by shifting renewable electricity across time and supporting reserve provision, but its contribution depends on charging and discharging limits, state-of-charge dynamics, and the timing of system needs [16]–[21]. As a result, annual renewable-energy shares alone cannot quantify the actual operating cost, generator schedules, curtailment volume, battery utilization, carbon dioxide emissions, or environmental consequences of high-renewable scenarios.

The environmental dimension further strengthens this policy-operation gap. Renewable energy expansion does not automatically guarantee minimized environmental impact if thermal backup is inefficiently scheduled or if curtailment, reserve provision, and battery operation are not optimally coordinated. A system with a high renewable share may still require frequent thermal generator startups, part-load operation, or backup generation during low-renewable periods. Such operation can increase fuel consumption, operating cost, carbon dioxide emissions, and life cycle environmental damage. Moreover, renewable technologies and battery systems have nonzero life cycle impacts associated with materials, manufacturing, installation, maintenance, and end-of-life treatment [22]–[30]. Therefore, environmental evaluation based only on annual generation shares or direct operational emissions may not fully represent the real environmental outcome of power system operation. A more comprehensive framework is required to connect annual policy targets with hourly dispatch and life cycle assessment.

Unit Commitment is one of the main optimization approaches used for short-term power system scheduling. Conventional Unit Commitment models determine the on/off status and output of generating units while minimizing operating cost under technical constraints such as power balance, generation limits, ramping limits, startup and shutdown logic, minimum up/down time, and reserve requirements [31]–[35]. These models provide the computational foundation for practical generation scheduling and are commonly formulated as mixed-integer linear programming problems. However, conventional cost-based Unit Commitment mainly evaluates economic operation. Broader life cycle environmental damage is usually not included directly in the scheduling objective.

Security-constrained Unit Commitment extends the basic framework by considering network security, transmission constraints, and contingency-related requirements, which are important for reliable operation under possible outages or transmission limitations [36]–[38]. Renewable-integrated Unit Commitment studies further consider wind and photovoltaic uncertainty, reserve scheduling, stochastic or robust optimization, and renewable curtailment [3], [4], [39]–[42]. These studies show that higher renewable penetration increases the need for flexibility and reserve coordination. Battery-energy-storage Unit Commitment studies have also demonstrated that storage can reduce operating cost, improve renewable utilization, and provide flexibility services when optimally scheduled [16]–[21]. Nevertheless, most of these studies focus on operating cost, reliability, uncertainty, or direct emissions. The life cycle environmental damage associated with hourly dispatch decisions is often evaluated outside the optimization model or omitted from the scheduling objective.

Multi-objective Unit Commitment studies have attempted to address environmental operation by minimizing both cost and emissions or by constructing trade-offs between economic and environmental objectives [43], [44]. These studies are important because they show that cost-minimizing operation and emission-minimizing operation can lead to different generator schedules. However, environmental objectives in many Unit Commitment models are commonly represented by direct carbon dioxide emissions or fuel-based emission factors. These indicators are useful, but they do not capture the full life cycle burdens of electricity generation technologies. Life Cycle Assessment provides a broader environmental evaluation by considering impacts from fuel extraction, fuel transportation, equipment manufacturing, construction, operation, maintenance, and end-of-life processes [22]–[30]. LCA studies of electricity generation technologies have shown that coal, oil, natural gas, photovoltaic generation, wind power, hydropower, and battery systems differ substantially across climate change and other impact categories [22]–[30]. However, many LCA studies use average generation factors or scenario-level comparisons and do not optimize hourly generator commitment and dispatch.

Policy scenario studies are also relevant to this research. Energy policy and energy system modeling studies evaluate long-term decarbonization pathways, renewable deployment levels, electricity demand, energy security, and future power generation mixes [1], [2], [5]–[15]. These studies are essential for defining national transition pathways and policy targets. However, policy scenario analysis generally operates at an annual or long-term planning scale and does not fully represent the hourly operational constraints of individual generating units, battery storage dynamics, curtailment decisions, or life cycle environmental damage caused by actual dispatch. Consequently, the gap between policy targets and real operational and environmental outcomes remains insufficiently quantified.

This paper addresses this gap by proposing a policy-aligned Unit Commitment–Life Cycle Assessment optimization framework for renewable energy integration in Japan. The framework translates annual policy-oriented scenarios into hourly operational schedules and evaluates their economic and environmental consequences under Unit Commitment constraints. The proposed model minimizes a weighted objective composed of operating cost and life cycle environmental damage. Thermal generators, photovoltaic generation, wind power, hydropower, and battery energy storage are scheduled while satisfying operational constraints, including power balance, generation limits, ramping limits, startup and shutdown behavior, minimum up/down time, reserve requirements, renewable curtailment, and battery state-of-charge dynamics.

The main novelty lies in embedding life cycle environmental damage directly into a policy-aligned Unit Commitment objective, enabling annual energy-policy targets to be evaluated through hourly operational schedules and environmental outcomes. Unlike policy studies that mainly evaluate annual generation shares, and unlike conventional Unit Commitment studies that focus primarily on operating cost or direct emissions, the proposed framework links energy-policy targets, operational feasibility, and life cycle assessment in a single optimization model. This integration makes it possible to examine how renewable expansion affects thermal generator operation, renewable curtailment, battery dispatch, carbon dioxide emissions, operating cost, life cycle assessment impacts, and total environmental damage.

The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows. First, the study establishes a policy-to-operation linkage by translating Japanese energy-policy targets into hourly Unit Commitment scenarios. Second, life cycle environmental damage is incorporated directly into the Unit Commitment objective rather than being evaluated only after cost minimization. Third, actual, fiscal year 2040-oriented, and net-zero-oriented scenarios are evaluated under a unified optimization framework, enabling consistent comparison of operating cost, emissions, curtailment, battery operation, and environmental damage. Fourth, the framework identifies compromise schedules that balance operating cost reduction, renewable integration, and life cycle environmental damage reduction.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section II presents the proposed Unit Commitment–Life Cycle Assessment formulation. Section III describes the system data, generation technologies, technology-specific life cycle environmental damage coefficients, and policy-aligned scenarios. Section IV presents the simulation results and compares the actual, fiscal year 2040-oriented, and net-zero-oriented cases. Section V discusses the operational and environmental implications of the results. Section VI concludes the paper and summarizes future research directions.
